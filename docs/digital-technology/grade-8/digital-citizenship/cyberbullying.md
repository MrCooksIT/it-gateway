# Cyberbullying

## What Is Cyberbullying?

:::tip Key Term
**Cyberbullying** is the use of electronic communication to bully a person — including sending, posting, or sharing negative, harmful, false, or mean content about someone else.
:::

Cyberbullying is not just unkind behaviour online. It is a serious form of harassment that affects real people in very real ways. In many states and countries, cyberbullying is considered a **criminal offence**.

---

## Why Cyberbullying Is Especially Harmful

It might be tempting to think that online behaviour is less harmful than face-to-face behaviour — after all, it is "just the internet." This thinking is wrong. Cyberbullying hurts real people. Victims may experience **isolation**, **low self-esteem**, and **depression**.

There are three characteristics that make cyberbullying particularly harmful compared to traditional bullying:

| Characteristic | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Persistent** | Digital devices allow constant communication, so victims have no respite. A bully can continue the harassment at home, after school hours, and at any time of day or night — the victim never gets a break. |
| **Permanent** | Cyberbullying contributes to the digital footprint of both the victim and the bully. Incidents can follow both parties long after the behaviour stops. |
| **Discreet** | Teachers and parents cannot see or overhear cyberbullying the way they might witness physical bullying. This makes it difficult for adults to intervene and stop the behaviour. |

---

## The Four Roles in Cyberbullying

Every cyberbullying situation involves people playing different roles. Understanding these roles is important because it helps you recognise where you stand and what you can do.

:::info The Four Roles
**Bully** — The person who uses digital devices to harass and hurt the victim.

**Victim** — The person who is being cyberbullied.

**Bystander** — A person who is aware that cyberbullying is happening but does not intervene. Bystanders sometimes stay silent because they are afraid of becoming a target themselves.

**Upstander** — A person who tries to break the cycle of cyberbullying by standing up for the victim or asking for help.
:::

---

## What to Do If You Are Being Cyberbullied

If you are the victim of cyberbullying, there are clear steps you should follow:

### Do

- **Do not respond to harassment or threats.** Responding — even to defend yourself — often encourages the bully by giving them the reaction they want.
- **Keep a record of messages received.** Print or take screenshots so that you have evidence of the bullying, even if the bully later deletes it.
- **Reach out to a trusted adult.** Tell a parent, teacher, counsellor, or school administrator. These people have the power to help stop the bullying.
- **Block communication from the bully.** Depending on the platform, you may be able to block the bully from contacting you.
- **Report bullies to the website or platform administrator.** Bullying and harassment are almost always a violation of a platform's terms of use. Reporting can result in posts being removed or the bully being suspended or banned from the platform.

### Do Not

- **Do not retaliate by bullying the bully.** This only brings you down to their level and could get you in trouble as well.
- **Do not forward messages from the bully.** You cannot control where the message will spread once you share it.
- **Do not believe the bully.** What a bully says tells you more about them than it does about you. Try not to let their behaviour affect how you see yourself. If the bullying has affected your self-esteem, reach out to trusted friends and adults for support.

---

## What to Do If You See Cyberbullying

Witnessing cyberbullying and doing nothing makes you a **bystander** — and bystanders allow bullying to continue. Instead, choose to be an **upstander** and break the cycle.

### How to Be an Upstander

- **Reach out to the victim.** Let them know that you care, that the bully is wrong, and encourage them to seek help from a trusted adult.
- **Do not bully the person who is being a bully.** This does not help the situation and you can get into trouble for it.
- **Tell a teacher, parent, or counsellor** about the bullying. Cyberbullying is serious and adults need to be involved.
- **Report the bullying to the platform administrator** to get harmful posts removed.

:::warning Do Not Be a Bystander
Staying silent when you witness cyberbullying allows it to continue. Even a small act — privately telling the victim you support them, or quietly telling a trusted adult — can make a real difference.
:::

## Summary

| Concept | Key Point |
|---|---|
| Definition | Using electronic communication to bully someone — sending, posting, or sharing negative, harmful, false, or mean content |
| Why it is serious | It is persistent (no escape), permanent (leaves a digital footprint), and discreet (hard for adults to detect) |
| Effects on victims | Isolation, low self-esteem, depression |
| The four roles | Bully, victim, bystander, upstander |
| If you are bullied | Do not respond; keep records; block; report; tell a trusted adult |
| If you witness it | Be an upstander — support the victim and involve a trusted adult |
| Legal note | Cyberbullying is a crime in many places |
