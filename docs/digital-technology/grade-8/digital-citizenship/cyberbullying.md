# Cyberbullying

## Introduction

Bullying is not a new problem. For as long as people have gathered in communities — playgrounds, classrooms, workplaces — some have used their power to hurt others. What has changed dramatically in the last two decades is the environment in which bullying occurs. Bullying has followed young people into their homes, onto their phones, and into every moment of their day. This is **cyberbullying**, and it is one of the most serious challenges facing young people in the digital age.

This chapter explains what cyberbullying is, why it is more harmful than many people assume, and exactly what you should do if you encounter it — whether as a victim, a witness, or someone who has been accused.

---

## What Is Cyberbullying?

:::tip Key Term
**Cyberbullying** is the repeated use of digital technology — including social media, messaging apps, gaming platforms, and email — to deliberately harm, harass, threaten, humiliate, or embarrass another person.
:::

The key word in that definition is **repeated**. A single unkind comment, while still wrong, is not necessarily bullying. Cyberbullying involves a pattern of behaviour intended to cause ongoing harm to the victim.

### How Cyberbullying Differs from Traditional Bullying

Traditional bullying and cyberbullying share the same cruel intention, but they are different in important ways:

| Feature | Traditional Bullying | Cyberbullying |
|---|---|---|
| **Where it happens** | Physical spaces (school, playground) | Online, on any device, anywhere |
| **Time** | Limited to when people are in the same place | 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — no escape |
| **Audience** | Usually limited (classmates, bystanders present) | Potentially millions of people worldwide |
| **Anonymity** | Bully is usually known to victim | Bully can hide behind a fake identity |
| **Permanence** | Spoken words are forgotten; physical acts fade | Written posts, images, and videos last forever |
| **Evidence** | Often no physical evidence | Can be screenshotted and saved |

This table shows why cyberbullying is, in many ways, worse than traditional bullying. The victim cannot escape it by going home. The audience is unlimited. The content may never disappear.

## Types of Cyberbullying

Cyberbullying takes many forms. It is important to be able to recognise all of them — not just the obvious ones.

### 1. Harassment

Sending repeated, offensive, threatening, or hurtful messages directly to someone. This can happen through text messages, WhatsApp, DMs on social media, or in-game chat.

**Example:** Sending daily messages to a classmate saying "nobody likes you" or "you should leave our school."

### 2. Impersonation

Creating a fake account pretending to be someone else in order to damage their reputation, post embarrassing content, or trick others. This is also sometimes called **identity theft** in an online context.

**Example:** Creating a fake Instagram account with someone's photos and posting offensive content that makes it look like that person said or did something they didn't.

### 3. Outing (Disclosure of Private Information)

Sharing someone's private, sensitive, or embarrassing information online without their permission. This could include private photos, secrets told in confidence, personal struggles, or other information the person shared privately.

:::warning Important
Sharing someone's private images — especially intimate ones — without their consent is not just cyberbullying. In South Africa, it is a criminal offence under the Cybercrimes Act.
:::

### 4. Exclusion

Deliberately excluding someone from an online group, chat, or game in a way that is intended to make them feel rejected or isolated.

**Example:** Creating a group chat that includes every member of a friend group except one person, specifically to leave them out and make them aware of it.

### 5. Cyberstalking

Repeatedly tracking, monitoring, or harassing someone online. This may include sending threatening messages, tracking someone's location, or gathering and using personal information to frighten them.

### 6. Fraping

Accessing someone else's social media account without their permission and posting content as if you are them. The word comes from a combination of "Facebook" and an offensive term, but it applies to any platform.

**Example:** While a classmate's phone is left unlocked, someone logs into their Instagram and posts embarrassing or offensive content.

:::danger Critical Warning
Accessing another person's account without permission is a criminal offence under South Africa's Cybercrimes Act — even if you think it is "just a joke." This is not a grey area. It is illegal.
:::

### 7. Trolling

Deliberately posting provocative, hurtful, or offensive content online to upset people or cause conflict. Trolls often target public posts but can also target individuals.

### 8. Catfishing

Creating a false online identity — often using someone else's photos and a made-up name — to deceive another person, usually in a romantic context. The person being deceived believes they are forming a genuine friendship or relationship.

**Example:** Someone creates a fake profile pretending to be an attractive person their own age, gains a classmate's trust over several months, then uses that trust to extract personal information or cause emotional harm.

### 9. Flaming

Sending angry, hostile, and insulting messages in an online argument. Unlike harassment, flaming often involves both parties attacking each other, but it can quickly escalate into one-sided bullying.

## Why Cyberbullying Is Serious

Some people dismiss cyberbullying as "not real" or less serious than face-to-face bullying because it happens "only online." This is deeply mistaken.

### The 24/7 Nature

Traditional bullying ends when you go home. Cyberbullying follows you everywhere. For many young people, their phone is the last thing they check before sleeping and the first thing they check in the morning. If that phone is a source of cruel messages, there is no safe space — not even your bedroom.

### The Scale of the Audience

A single embarrassing post can be shared hundreds or thousands of times within hours. The victim's humiliation is not confined to their class or school — it can spread across the country and the internet, reaching people they have never met.

### Anonymity

The internet allows bullies to hide behind fake names and profiles. This anonymity can make bullies bolder and more cruel than they would be face-to-face. It can also make the victim feel helpless because they do not know who is attacking them.

### Permanence

Hurtful words spoken in the schoolyard are forgotten over time. Hurtful words posted online — screenshots, cached pages, shares and reposts — can last for years or forever.

## Effects on Victims

Cyberbullying has real, serious consequences for the people who experience it.

:::info Research Finding
Studies consistently show that victims of cyberbullying are significantly more likely to experience depression, anxiety, and low self-esteem than those who have not been bullied. In serious cases, cyberbullying has been linked to self-harm and suicide.
:::

The effects on victims can include:

- **Mental health:** Anxiety, depression, feelings of shame and worthlessness
- **Academic performance:** Difficulty concentrating, declining grades, school avoidance
- **Social isolation:** Withdrawal from friends, family, and activities they previously enjoyed
- **Physical health:** Sleep problems, headaches, appetite changes
- **Loss of trust:** Difficulty trusting others, fear of using technology

It is important to understand that these are not signs of weakness in the victim. They are normal responses to a situation of ongoing harm and helplessness.

## Warning Signs That Someone Is Being Cyberbullied

People who are being cyberbullied often do not tell anyone — out of shame, fear of making things worse, or not wanting to worry adults. As a friend, it is useful to know the warning signs:

- Appearing upset or anxious after using their phone or computer
- Becoming secretive about their online activity
- Avoiding school or social situations
- Unexplained changes in mood or behaviour
- Withdrawing from friends and family
- Loss of interest in activities they previously enjoyed
- Stopping use of social media or messaging apps suddenly

If you notice these signs in a friend, gently let them know you are there to support them.

## What to Do If You Are Being Cyberbullied

### Step 1: Do Not Respond

Responding to a bully — whether by defending yourself, insulting them back, or begging them to stop — typically makes things worse. Bullies want a reaction. Denying them that reaction removes their power.

### Step 2: Block and Report

Every major platform has tools to block users and report abusive content. Use them.

| Platform | How to Report |
|---|---|
| Instagram | Tap the three dots on a post or profile > Report |
| WhatsApp | Long press a message > Report |
| TikTok | Press and hold a comment or tap "..." on a profile > Report |
| YouTube | Click three dots on a comment > Report |
| Facebook | Click "..." on a post or profile > Find Support or Report |

Reporting does not always result in immediate action, but it creates a record and helps platforms identify persistent abusers.

### Step 3: Save the Evidence

:::warning Do This First
Before you block someone, take screenshots of the bullying content. Note the date, time, and platform. This evidence may be important if you need to report the bullying to a teacher, parent, or the police.
:::

### Step 4: Tell a Trusted Adult

This is the most important step, and the one that many young people are most reluctant to take. You should tell a parent, guardian, teacher, or school counsellor. Adults have more tools to help than you might think. They can contact the school, the platform, or law enforcement if necessary.

If you feel you cannot tell an adult in your life, contact a support organisation:
- **SAPS (South African Police Service):** 10111
- **ChildLine South Africa:** 0800 055 555 (free, 24 hours)

## Being an Upstander, Not a Bystander

:::tip Key Term
A **bystander** is someone who witnesses bullying but does nothing. An **upstander** is someone who witnesses bullying and takes action to stop it or support the victim.
:::

If you witness cyberbullying happening to someone else, you have a choice. Doing nothing — scrolling past, laughing along, or ignoring it — makes you a bystander, and bystanders enable bullying to continue. Being an upstander means:

- **Not liking or sharing** content that is being used to bully someone
- **Reporting** the post or profile to the platform
- **Reaching out privately** to the victim to let them know you support them
- **Telling an adult** — a teacher, parent, or school counsellor
- **Refusing to participate** even if your friend group is doing it

You do not have to publicly confront the bully yourself — especially if doing so might put you at risk. Even a private message to the victim saying "I saw what happened and I'm sorry, are you okay?" can make an enormous difference.

## What to Do If You Are Accused of Cyberbullying

Sometimes people cross a line without fully realising it. If someone tells you that your messages, posts, or comments have hurt them, take it seriously:

1. **Stop the behaviour immediately** — do not continue, even if you think the person is overreacting.
2. **Reflect honestly** — consider whether your words were kind, necessary, and true.
3. **Apologise sincerely** — a genuine, private apology (not a public one designed to look good) is appropriate.
4. **Do not make excuses** — "I was joking" or "they're too sensitive" are not acceptable responses to causing harm.
5. **Think about why it happened** — is there something in your own behaviour or thinking that needs to change?

## Cyberbullying and the Law: The Cybercrimes Act

:::tip Key Term
The **Cybercrimes Act 19 of 2020** is South African legislation that criminalises a wide range of online behaviours, including those related to cyberbullying and harassment.
:::

The Cybercrimes Act makes the following activities criminal offences:

- **Cyberstalking:** Sending messages that cause a person to fear for their safety
- **Harassment:** Repeatedly contacting someone in a way that causes them distress
- **Distribution of private sexual images:** Sharing intimate images without consent
- **Unlawful access to computer systems:** Accessing someone's accounts without permission (fraping)

A conviction under the Cybercrimes Act can result in a fine or imprisonment. Being young does not make you immune — minors can be charged under the Act, and parents or guardians can also face consequences.

:::warning Legal Note
"I didn't mean it" and "it was just a joke" are not legal defences. If your actions caused harm — or intended to cause harm — you can be held legally responsible.
:::

## School Policies on Cyberbullying

Even though cyberbullying often happens outside of school hours and off school premises, most South African schools have policies that allow them to respond to cyberbullying that affects the school community. This means that bullying on social media between learners can result in school disciplinary action, even if it happened on a weekend at home.

Schools typically have:
- A code of conduct that covers online behaviour
- A process for reporting cyberbullying to staff
- Consequences ranging from counselling to suspension for serious cases

Know your school's policy and use the official reporting channels available to you.
