# Digital Footprint & Reputation

## Introduction

Imagine walking across a muddy field. Every step you take leaves a footprint behind. You cannot un-step those steps — the prints are there, and anyone who walks past can see where you have been.

The internet works in a very similar way. Every time you go online — whether you are searching, posting, messaging, watching a video, or even just visiting a website — you leave behind a trace. This trace is called your **digital footprint**, and it is far more detailed and permanent than most people realise.

Understanding your digital footprint is one of the most important digital literacy skills you can develop. It affects your reputation today and can have consequences far into your future.

---

## What Is a Digital Footprint?

:::tip Key Term
A **digital footprint** is the trail of data that is created and stored whenever you use the internet or digital devices. It includes everything from the websites you visit and the posts you publish, to the apps you use and the location data your phone collects.
:::

Your digital footprint is not just one thing — it is a combination of many different types of data, collected in many different ways. Think of it like a puzzle: each piece on its own might seem harmless or trivial, but together they form a surprisingly detailed picture of who you are, where you go, what you believe, and how you behave.

---

## Active vs Passive Digital Footprints

Your digital footprint has two main parts: **active** and **passive**. It is important to understand the difference.

### Active Digital Footprint

:::tip Key Term
An **active digital footprint** is the data you deliberately and knowingly create when you participate online. This is the content you choose to put out into the world.
:::

Examples of your active digital footprint include:

- **Social media posts:** Everything you post on Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, X (formerly Twitter), or any other platform
- **Comments:** Comments you leave on posts, videos (e.g. YouTube), or articles
- **Profile information:** Your name, age, school, interests, and profile photo that you fill in when creating an account
- **Emails and messages:** Messages you send, even through "disappearing" message features
- **Reviews and ratings:** Product reviews, ratings on apps, restaurant reviews
- **Forum posts:** Contributions to Reddit, Quora, Discord servers, or any online discussion
- **Photos and videos you upload:** Even if you later delete them, they may have already been saved or shared

The key characteristic of your active footprint is that **you made a choice to create it**. You typed the words, you uploaded the photo, you clicked "post." This means you have more control over it — but also more responsibility for it.

### Passive Digital Footprint

:::tip Key Term
A **passive digital footprint** is data that is collected about you without you actively contributing it — and often without you even being aware it is happening.
:::

Examples of your passive digital footprint include:

- **Browsing history:** Every website your browser visits is logged — by your browser, your internet service provider, and often the websites themselves
- **Location data:** Apps on your phone constantly track and record your physical location, even when you are not actively using them
- **Cookies:** Small files websites place on your device to remember who you are and track your behaviour across the internet
- **Search history:** Every search you type into Google, Bing, or any search engine is recorded and linked to your account or device
- **App usage:** How long you spend on each app, which features you use, and when you use them is tracked by app developers
- **Purchases:** What you buy, when, and from where is recorded by banks, shopping apps, and payment services
- **Likes and time spent:** Even if you don't click "like" on a post, platforms track how long you paused on it while scrolling

:::info Did You Know?
Even when you use "Incognito Mode" or "Private Browsing," your internet service provider, school network, and the websites you visit can still see what you are doing. Incognito mode only prevents your browser from saving a local history on your own device.
:::

## How Your Digital Footprint Forms Your Online Reputation

Your **online reputation** is the impression that others form of you based on what they can find about you online. Just as your behaviour at school builds your reputation among teachers and classmates, your behaviour online builds your reputation in digital spaces — and beyond.

The problem is that your online reputation is not only visible to your friends. It can be seen by:

- **Future employers** who search your name before offering you a job
- **University admissions staff** who research applicants
- **Coaches, clubs, and organisations** you apply to join
- **Journalists and the public** if you ever become newsworthy
- **Strangers** who might interact with you online
- **People with harmful intentions** who could use your information against you

### What Your Digital Footprint Says About You

Consider two learners applying for the same university bursary. Both have similar marks. The admissions officer searches their names online.

- **Learner A:** Has no public social media profiles. A search reveals their name on the school's honour roll page and a comment they made on a community forum helping someone with a maths problem.
- **Learner B:** Has a public Instagram filled with posts mocking classmates, several Twitter/X arguments with aggressive language, and a photo at a party with alcohol.

Both are 16 years old. Which learner is more likely to receive the bursary?

Your online presence communicates your values, your maturity, and your character — whether you intend it to or not.

## Real-World Consequences

The following examples are based on real types of situations that occur regularly. Names have been changed.

### Scenario 1: The Deleted Post That Wasn't

**Aisha** was a 17-year-old with excellent academic results who applied to a well-known university. During her school years, she had made some offensive jokes on Twitter that she thought were funny at the time. She deleted the tweets when she was 16. What she did not know was that several people had screenshot the posts and shared them. The university found them, and her application was declined. She was told her "values did not align with the institution's."

**Lesson:** Deleting content does not erase it. Screenshots, caches, and sharing mean that anything posted online can outlast your decision to remove it.

### Scenario 2: The Gaming Username

**Sipho** used an inappropriate, offensive username in an online game. He thought it was funny and harmless because it was "just a game." Years later, when applying for an internship at a technology company, the recruiter searched his email address (which he had used to register the gaming account) and found his gaming profile — including the offensive username and in-game chat logs that were publicly visible. The internship offer was withdrawn.

**Lesson:** Usernames and online identities are part of your digital footprint, even in gaming and entertainment contexts.

## The Permanence of Online Content

:::warning Important
**Nothing you post online is ever truly deleted.** This is one of the most important facts to understand about the internet.
:::

Here is why deleted content can still exist:

1. **Screenshots:** Anyone who saw your post before you deleted it could have taken a screenshot and saved it to their device or shared it further.
2. **The Wayback Machine:** The Internet Archive (web.archive.org) takes regular snapshots of websites and pages, including social media profiles that were set to public.
3. **Google cache:** Google stores cached (saved) versions of web pages, which may still be accessible even after the original is deleted.
4. **Server backups:** Social media platforms keep backup copies of your data on their servers even after you think you have deleted it. Many platforms' own terms of service confirm that your data is retained for extended periods.
5. **Third-party apps:** If you ever granted access to a third-party app (like a quiz app or a login-with-Google service), they may have copies of your data.
6. **Re-sharing and reposts:** Content that has been shared, retweeted, or reposted by others lives on their profiles, independent of your own.

The internet's memory is far longer than human memory. Before you post anything, ask yourself: **Would I be comfortable with my teacher, my parent, a future employer, or the whole world seeing this forever?**

## How to Manage Your Digital Footprint

Managing your digital footprint is an ongoing process, not a once-off task. Here are practical strategies.

### 1. Google Yourself

Type your own name into a search engine. See what comes up. Try different combinations — your full name, your name and your school, your name and your town. What would someone else see if they searched for you? Repeat this regularly.

### 2. Audit Your Accounts

Make a list of every online account you have — social media, gaming, shopping, streaming, school tools, apps. For each one, ask:
- Is this account still active and necessary?
- What personal information is visible?
- Who can see my posts or profile?
- Is my password strong and unique?

Delete accounts you no longer use. The fewer places your data lives, the better.

### 3. Review and Update Privacy Settings

Every major platform has privacy settings that control who can see your content. The default settings are almost always set to "public" — which means anyone can see your information. Change this.

| Platform | Where to Find Privacy Settings |
|---|---|
| Instagram | Settings > Privacy |
| TikTok | Settings and Privacy > Privacy |
| Facebook | Settings > Privacy Checkup |
| YouTube | Settings > Privacy |
| Snapchat | Settings > Privacy Controls |

Set your profile to private. Limit who can comment on your posts. Disable location sharing unless you actively need it. Review which third-party apps have access to your account and revoke any that are unnecessary.

### 4. Think Before You Post

Develop a personal rule: **pause before posting.** Ask yourself:
- Is this kind?
- Is this true?
- Would I say this to someone's face?
- Would I be embarrassed if my parents saw this?
- Could this hurt someone?
- Could this hurt me in the future?

If the answer to any of these raises doubt, do not post it.

### 5. Use Thoughtful Usernames

Your username is part of your digital identity. Avoid usernames that are offensive, inappropriate, or revealing of personal information (like your real name, school, or birth year). Choose something that you would not mind appearing in a search result.

### 6. Be Careful with Location Data

Avoid sharing your real-time location online. Posting "I'm at the mall right now" or tagging your exact home address in a photo gives strangers information about your physical whereabouts. Many phones embed location data (GPS coordinates) in photos — consider disabling this in your camera settings.

## Case Study: A Footprint to Be Proud Of

Not all digital footprints are damaging. A well-managed online presence can actually **help** you.

**Naledi** is a Grade 11 learner who runs a small crafts business selling handmade jewellery. She posts photos of her work on Instagram and responds warmly to customers. She blogs occasionally about the creative process. She has commented thoughtfully on several design forums. When a local journalist writes an article about young entrepreneurs, they find Naledi through her positive online presence and feature her in the story.

When Naledi later applies for a design bursary, the selection committee finds her professional Instagram page, her helpful forum comments, and the newspaper article. Her digital footprint tells a story of creativity, work ethic, and good character.

**Lesson:** Your digital footprint is not just a risk to manage — it is also an opportunity to build. You can deliberately create a positive digital identity.
