# Digital Citizenship & Cyber Hygiene

## Unit Overview

Every time you search something online, post a photo, play a game with strangers, or send a message, you are participating in a vast digital world. Like the physical world, the digital world has rules, responsibilities, and risks. **Digital citizenship** is about understanding those rules and responsibilities — and making smart, ethical choices online.

This unit draws on the CodeHS Digital Citizenship & Cyber Hygiene framework and aligns with the South African Grade 8 Digital Technology curriculum. By the end of this unit, you will not only know how to stay safe online, but also how to act with integrity, respect, and purpose in every digital interaction.

---

## What Is a Digital Citizen?

A **digital citizen** is anyone who uses the internet and digital technology as part of their daily life. That includes you. Being a *good* digital citizen means:

- Treating others online with the same respect you would show in person
- Protecting your own personal information and privacy
- Thinking critically about the information you find online
- Understanding your rights and responsibilities under the law
- Contributing positively to online communities

:::info Why This Matters
South Africa has over 45 million internet users. Young people aged 15–24 are the most active online group in the country. The choices you make online today can follow you for years — into high school, university applications, and your professional career.
:::

---

## Why Digital Citizenship Matters

Consider these facts about life in the digital age:

- **Employers search candidates online.** Over 70% of employers check job applicants' social media profiles before hiring. Posts from years ago can affect your career before it even starts.
- **University admissions panels look at applicants' online presence.** A single controversial post can cost a learner their acceptance.
- **Cyberbullying is widespread.** Studies suggest that 1 in 3 young people globally have experienced cyberbullying. In South Africa, the numbers are similar.
- **Online scams cost South Africans billions.** The South African Banking Risk Information Centre (SABRIC) reports that digital fraud costs South Africans billions of rand each year.
- **Data is the most valuable commodity in the world.** Understanding how your data is collected and used is essential knowledge for the 21st century.

Digital citizenship is not just a school subject. It is a life skill. The habits you build now — around privacy, respect, critical thinking, and security — will serve you for the rest of your life.

:::info A Note for Learners
You are growing up in a world that your parents and teachers did not grow up in. The internet did not exist in its current form for most of their childhoods. That means this is an area where *you* have to take responsibility for your own learning and choices — because the adults around you are still learning too.
:::

## Unit Topics

This unit is divided into eight chapters. Each chapter covers a key area of digital citizenship. Below is a summary of what you will learn in each one.

---

### 1. Digital Footprint & Reputation

**File:** [digital-footprint.md](./digital-footprint.md)

Every action you take online — every post, like, search, and login — creates a record. This record is your **digital footprint**. In this chapter you will learn the difference between your active footprint (things you choose to share) and your passive footprint (data collected about you without you realising). You will also explore how your digital footprint shapes your online reputation and what practical steps you can take to manage it responsibly.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define digital footprint and distinguish between active and passive footprints
- Explain how a digital footprint affects a person's reputation
- Apply strategies to manage and protect your digital footprint

---

### 2. Cyberbullying

**File:** [cyberbullying.md](./cyberbullying.md)

Cyberbullying is bullying that takes place through digital devices — phones, computers, tablets. It can happen via social media, messaging apps, games, and email. This chapter covers the many forms cyberbullying takes, why it is more harmful than many people realise, and exactly what you should do if you experience, witness, or are accused of cyberbullying. South African law on this topic is also covered.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define cyberbullying and list its different forms
- Explain why cyberbullying is serious and its effects on victims
- Describe appropriate responses for victims, bystanders, and those accused
- Reference the South African Cybercrimes Act in relation to online harassment

---

### 3. Internet Safety

**File:** [internet-safety.md](./internet-safety.md)

The internet opens doors to enormous amounts of information and connection — but it also exposes users to real dangers. This chapter covers the personal information you should never share online, how online predators and scammers operate, how to recognise phishing attempts, and how to stay safe while gaming, browsing, and communicating online.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Identify categories of personal information that should not be shared online
- Recognise the warning signs of online grooming, phishing, and scams
- Describe safety practices for social media, gaming, and public Wi-Fi
- Explain how and where to report unsafe or harmful online content

---

### 4. Privacy & Security

**File:** [privacy-security.md](./privacy-security.md)

Privacy and security work together to protect you online. Privacy is your right to control your personal information. Security is the set of tools and habits that protect your devices and accounts from being accessed without permission. This chapter covers strong passwords, two-factor authentication, what data websites collect about you, identity theft, and what to do if your data is leaked in a breach.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Distinguish between privacy and cybersecurity
- Create strong, secure passwords and explain why they matter
- Explain how two-factor authentication works and why it adds security
- Describe what data websites collect and how it is used
- Define identity theft and outline prevention strategies

---

### 5. Information Literacy

**File:** [information-literacy.md](./information-literacy.md)

Not everything you read online is true. In fact, a significant amount of what circulates on social media is misleading, exaggerated, or completely false. This chapter teaches you how to evaluate sources critically using the CRAAP test, how to recognise fake news, how to understand bias, and how filter bubbles affect what information reaches you.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define misinformation, disinformation, and malinformation
- Apply the CRAAP test to evaluate online sources
- Use reverse image search and cross-referencing to verify claims
- Explain how filter bubbles and echo chambers form
- Cite digital sources correctly and uphold academic integrity

---

### 6. Creative Credit & Copyright

**File:** [creative-credit-copyright.md](./creative-credit-copyright.md)

When someone creates something — a song, a photograph, a piece of writing, a video — the law gives them rights over that creation. This chapter explains copyright, what it protects, and how long it lasts. You will also learn about Creative Commons licences, where to find free-to-use content legally, and why plagiarism and piracy are both wrong and have real consequences.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define copyright and explain what it protects
- Identify the different Creative Commons licence types and their conditions
- Distinguish between plagiarism and legitimate research
- Find and correctly credit free-to-use digital content
- Explain why piracy is illegal and unethical

---

### 7. Hacking Ethics

**File:** [hacking-ethics.md](./hacking-ethics.md)

"Hacking" is a word that appears in movies and news headlines, but what does it actually mean? This chapter separates fact from fiction — exploring the difference between ethical hackers who help protect systems and criminals who break into them. You will also learn about common hacking techniques at a conceptual level, the growing field of cybersecurity as a career, and what South African law says about unauthorised access to computer systems.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Distinguish between white hat, black hat, and grey hat hackers
- Explain what ethical hacking and penetration testing involve
- Describe common attack types: phishing, social engineering, malware
- Identify common malware types and their effects
- Reference the South African Cybercrimes Act regarding hacking offences

---

## Learning Outcomes for the Full Unit

By the end of this unit, learners should be able to:

1. Demonstrate responsible and ethical behaviour in online environments
2. Protect their personal information and digital identity
3. Critically evaluate online information and digital media
4. Respond appropriately to cyberbullying, online harassment, and unsafe content
5. Understand the legal framework governing digital behaviour in South Africa
6. Respect intellectual property and give proper credit for digital content
7. Explain the basics of cybersecurity and the role of ethical hackers

- Complete the **Check Your Understanding** questions at the end of each chapter

Good luck — and remember: being smart online is just as important as being smart in the classroom.
