# Internet Safety

## Introduction

The internet is one of the most powerful tools ever created. It connects billions of people, provides access to almost unlimited information, and enables things that would have seemed like science fiction just thirty years ago. But that same openness — the fact that anyone anywhere can communicate with anyone else — also creates real dangers.

Internet safety means understanding those dangers and knowing how to protect yourself. It is not about being afraid of the internet. It is about being informed and sensible so that you can use it confidently without putting yourself at risk.

---

## What Is Internet Safety?

:::tip Key Term
**Internet safety** (also called online safety or e-safety) refers to the knowledge, skills, and habits that allow people to use the internet without exposing themselves to harm — including physical danger, emotional harm, financial loss, or damage to their privacy.
:::

Internet safety covers a wide range of situations: from the information you share in an online profile, to the emails you open, to the people you meet in games and chat rooms, to the links you click. Each of these situations carries risks that you can manage if you know what to look out for.

---

## Personal Information: What Never to Share Online

The most important rule of internet safety is understanding what information about yourself must be kept private. Some information, if shared with the wrong person, can put you in direct physical danger.

:::danger Never Share This Information Online
- **Your full name** — especially in combination with other details
- **Your home address or neighbourhood**
- **Your school name and location**
- **Your phone number**
- **Your passwords** — for anything, ever, to anyone
- **Your ID number or parent/guardian's ID number**
- **Your financial details** — bank accounts, card numbers, PINs
- **Your real-time location** — "I'm home alone" or "I'm at the mall right now"
- **Your daily routine** — "I walk home from school alone every day at 3pm"
:::

Why is this information dangerous? On its own, each piece might seem harmless. But a bad actor who knows your name, school, and the route you walk home has everything they need to find you in real life. This is why the combination of information matters as much as any individual piece.

### Think Before You Share: The Combination Problem

Consider this example:

> Your Instagram is public. You have posted:
> - A photo at your school sports day, tagged with your school's name
> - A photo of your bedroom window with a recognisable view of your street
> - A post saying "can't wait to walk home after school today — finally Friday!"
> - Your first and last name in your bio

Individually, none of these seem dangerous. Combined, a stranger now knows your full name, your school, what you look like, roughly where you live, and when you are likely to be alone.

## Online Predators: Grooming and Warning Signs

:::tip Key Term
An **online predator** is an adult who uses the internet to build a relationship with a young person for the purpose of manipulation, exploitation, or abuse.
:::

Online predators are not always easy to spot. They rarely present themselves as dangerous. Instead, they use a process called **grooming** to build trust over time.

### How Grooming Works

Grooming is a calculated process. It typically follows a pattern:

1. **Target selection:** The predator identifies a target — often a young person who appears lonely, unhappy, or who is sharing a lot of personal information publicly.
2. **Building a connection:** They make contact, often pretending to be someone their own age. They are friendly, attentive, and interested in everything about the young person.
3. **Gaining trust:** Over weeks or months, they build a friendship. They may offer sympathy, gifts (like in-game currency), or a sense of being understood.
4. **Isolating the victim:** They encourage the young person to keep the friendship secret and to spend more time with them than with real-life friends and family.
5. **Testing boundaries:** They begin introducing inappropriate topics gradually, making it seem normal.
6. **Exploitation:** Once they have the victim's trust and have isolated them, they exploit the relationship.

### Warning Signs of Grooming

- An online contact is excessively attentive and interested in you
- They quickly want to move the conversation to a private channel (from a game chat to WhatsApp, for example)
- They claim to be a teenager but seem unusually mature, knowledgeable, or wealthy
- They ask you not to tell your parents or friends about them
- They send you gifts (money, in-game items) for no apparent reason
- They make you feel special and like they are the only one who "really gets" you
- They ask you to share photos of yourself

:::danger If This Happens
If anyone online makes you feel uncomfortable, asks for photos, or suggests meeting in real life, tell a trusted adult immediately. You will not get in trouble. You have not done anything wrong. Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, it probably is.
:::

### What to Do

- **Stop contact immediately.** You do not owe anyone online your continued attention.
- **Do not delete the messages** — save them as evidence.
- **Tell a parent, guardian, or teacher right away.**
- **Report the profile** to the platform.
- **Contact the police** if you have been threatened, asked for inappropriate images, or are afraid.

## Social Media Safety

Social media platforms connect you with friends — and with strangers. Understanding how they work is essential.

### Age Restrictions

Most major social media platforms require users to be at least 13 years old. This is not an arbitrary rule — it exists partly because younger users are more vulnerable to the risks described in this chapter. If you are under the minimum age for a platform, consider whether it is appropriate for you to be using it.

### Privacy Settings

Every social media platform has privacy settings. The default setting is almost always **public**, which means anyone in the world can see your profile, posts, and location. Change this.

:::info Do This Now
Set your social media profiles to **private** so that only people you have approved can see your content. Review your settings after every major platform update — platforms sometimes reset privacy settings without warning.
:::

### Who Can See Your Posts?

Think about the following levels of visibility:

| Setting | Who Can See Your Posts |
|---|---|
| Public | Everyone in the world — including strangers, employers, predators |
| Friends of friends | Your friends AND everyone who is friends with any of your friends |
| Friends/Followers only | Only people you have approved |
| Only me | Only you |

Even on a private account, be thoughtful about what you post. Friends can screenshot and share. Accounts can be hacked. Screenshots can be leaked. Privacy settings reduce risk but do not eliminate it.

## Stranger Danger Online

You have probably been taught about "stranger danger" in the physical world — the idea that you should not accept rides from or go anywhere with people you don't know. The same principle applies online, but it is harder to apply because online relationships can feel very real.

### The Reality of Online Friendships

It is genuinely possible to form real friendships with people you meet online. Many people do. However, it is also impossible to verify who someone actually is online. A person who claims to be:
- A 14-year-old girl could be a 40-year-old man
- From your city could be from another country
- A student could be an adult with harmful intentions

This does not mean everyone online is lying. But it does mean you should never share personal information with, or agree to meet, someone you only know online — without first telling a trusted adult and bringing another adult with you.

:::warning Important Rule
**Never meet someone in person whom you only know online** without telling your parents or guardians and without taking an adult with you. If they ask you to keep the meeting secret, that is a major red flag.
:::

## Phishing: Fake Emails and Messages

:::tip Key Term
**Phishing** is a type of cybercrime where an attacker sends a fake email, message, or website that appears to come from a trusted source (like a bank, a social media platform, or even a friend) in order to trick you into revealing personal information or clicking a malicious link.
:::

The word "phishing" is a play on "fishing" — the attacker throws out bait and waits to see who bites.

### How to Spot a Phishing Attempt

Phishing messages often contain telltale signs:

| Red Flag | Example |
|---|---|
| **Urgent, alarming tone** | "Your account will be deleted in 24 hours!" |
| **Suspicious sender address** | The email says it's from PayPal but comes from paypa1-support@mail123.com |
| **Generic greeting** | "Dear User" instead of your actual name |
| **Requests for personal info** | "Please confirm your password and ID number" |
| **Suspicious links** | The link text says "bank.co.za" but hovering over it shows a different URL |
| **Spelling and grammar errors** | Professional companies proofread their communications |
| **Too good to be true** | "You have won R50,000!" |

### What to Do with a Suspected Phishing Message

1. **Do not click any links** in the message.
2. **Do not reply** to the message.
3. **Do not provide any information** it requests.
4. **Report it** to your email provider (most have a "Report phishing" button).
5. If it claims to be from your bank, **contact your bank directly** using the number on their official website — not the number in the suspicious message.

## Safe Online Gaming

Online gaming is enormously popular — and it comes with its own set of safety considerations.

### Do This / Don't Do This

| Do This | Don't Do This |
|---|---|
| Use a username that doesn't reveal your real name | Use your real name as your gamer tag |
| Play with voice chat disabled unless with known friends | Share personal details in voice chat with strangers |
| Report players who are abusive or inappropriate | Engage with players who make you uncomfortable |
| Use platform parental controls if relevant | Share your home address with online gaming friends |
| Tell a trusted adult if something worrying happens | Meet a gaming friend in real life without an adult present |

### In-Game Scams

Online games are full of scams targeting younger players:

- **"Free V-bucks/gems/coins" offers** that require you to enter your account details
- **Offers to "dupe" (duplicate) rare items** that are actually account theft attempts
- **Fake giveaways** requiring you to share your login credentials
- **Players offering gifts in exchange for personal information**

:::warning Rule of Thumb
If someone is offering you something in a game for free, especially in exchange for account details, it is almost certainly a scam. Legitimate game developers do not give away items through private messages.
:::

## Online Scams

Scams are not limited to email. They appear on social media, in WhatsApp messages, in gaming chats, and on websites.

### Common Scam Types

**"You've Won a Prize"**
> "Congratulations! You have been selected to receive a R10,000 Checkers voucher. Click here and enter your details to claim."

No legitimate competition selects winners at random from the general internet-browsing public. If you have to enter personal information to claim a prize you didn't enter to win, it is a scam.

**The Lottery Scam**
> "You have won the South African National Lottery. Please send your banking details to claim your prize."

You cannot win a lottery you didn't enter. No legitimate lottery asks for your banking details by email.

**Fake Job Offers**
> "Earn R5,000 per week working from home — no experience needed! Just pay a R500 registration fee to get started."

Legitimate employers do not charge you to work for them.

**Social Media Giveaways**
Fake accounts impersonating brands or celebrities promise prizes in exchange for follows, shares, and personal details. Check that the account is verified before engaging.

### The Golden Rule of Scams

:::danger If It Sounds Too Good to Be True, It Is
No one gives away money, prizes, or opportunities for free to random internet users. Any message that promises you unexpected financial gain in exchange for personal information or money is a scam. Always.
:::

## Reporting Unsafe Content

If you encounter content online that is illegal, harmful, threatening, or inappropriate, you have a responsibility — and the ability — to report it.

### On Social Media and Video Platforms

Every major platform has reporting tools built in. Reporting is anonymous and typically takes less than a minute.

| Situation | What to Do |
|---|---|
| Cyberbullying | Report the post/profile on the platform AND tell a school staff member |
| Illegal content (child abuse material, etc.) | Report to the platform AND to SAPS (10111) |
| Grooming or predatory behaviour | Report to the platform AND tell a trusted adult immediately |
| Threatening content | Screenshot, report to the platform, tell a parent or teacher |
| Dangerous misinformation | Report to the platform using the "false information" option |

### At School

Your school has its own reporting channels. Most schools have:
- A designated staff member (often a counsellor or a deputy principal) who handles technology-related complaints
- A code of conduct that covers online behaviour
- A process for escalating serious cases to parents or police

You should never feel alone in dealing with an unsafe online situation. Adults at your school and at home are there to help.

## Safe Passwords

Passwords are the locks on your digital life. A weak password is like leaving your front door unlocked.

:::tip Key Term
A **strong password** is one that is long, complex, and unique — meaning it is not used for any other account and does not contain easily guessable information like your name, birthday, or the word "password."
:::

### What Makes a Strong Password

| Feature | Example |
|---|---|
| **At least 12 characters long** | Longer is always better |
| **Mix of uppercase and lowercase letters** | `dOgHoUsE` rather than `doghouse` |
| **Numbers included** | `dOgH0us3` |
| **Special characters** | `d0gH@us3!` |
| **Not based on personal info** | Not your name, birthday, school, or pet's name |
| **Unique to this account** | Not the same password you use elsewhere |

A good technique is to use a **passphrase** — a string of random words that you can remember but that is very long. For example: `BlueFrogPlantsRocketShip42!` is both long and memorable.

### Password Managers

:::tip Key Term
A **password manager** is an app or browser tool that securely stores all your passwords, generates strong passwords for you, and fills them in automatically. You only need to remember one master password.
:::

Popular password managers include Bitwarden (free), 1Password, and Google Password Manager. Your school or parents may have guidance on which to use.

## Public Wi-Fi Dangers

Free public Wi-Fi — at shopping centres, restaurants, airports, and libraries — is convenient, but it carries risks.

:::warning Be Careful on Public Wi-Fi
On public Wi-Fi, others on the same network can potentially intercept your data. Avoid:
- Online banking or entering financial details
- Logging into accounts with sensitive information
- Entering passwords if the Wi-Fi is unsecured (no password required)

Use your mobile data for sensitive activities, or use a **VPN** (Virtual Private Network) if you must use public Wi-Fi.
:::

## Scenario: Spotting the Red Flags

Read the following scenario and identify all the safety concerns.

> **Scenario:** Zanele, 13, is playing an online game. A player named `GamerPro_SA` messages her privately, complimenting her skills. They chat in-game daily for two weeks. GamerPro_SA claims to be 15 and says his name is "Ryan." One day he asks if they can move the conversation to WhatsApp because "the in-game chat is annoying." He also mentions that he lives "not far from you" and that he'd love to meet up at the local mall to game together. He asks her not to tell her parents because "they'll make a big deal out of nothing."

**Red flags in this scenario:**
1. Moving to a private, external channel (WhatsApp)
2. Claiming to know where she lives (surveillance behaviour)
3. Suggesting an in-person meeting
4. Asking her to keep it secret from her parents
5. Building trust gradually over weeks before making requests

**What should Zanele do?** Stop responding, do not share her WhatsApp number, save the messages as evidence, and immediately tell a parent or trusted adult. She should also report the account on the gaming platform.

## Summary

| Topic | Key Rule |
|---|---|
| Personal information | Never share name, address, school, phone, passwords, or ID online |
| Online predators | Learn the grooming pattern; trust your instincts; tell an adult |
| Social media | Set to private; think before posting; check who can see what |
| Strangers online | Never meet alone; always tell a trusted adult first |
| Phishing | Check sender, don't click links, never provide personal info |
| Gaming | Use a pseudonym; never share personal details; report abuse |
| Scams | If it's too good to be true, it is |
