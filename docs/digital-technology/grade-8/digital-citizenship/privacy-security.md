# Privacy & Security

## The Digital Age

In the past five years alone, humans have created more data than ever before in history. This creates new challenges around protecting and securing that data — for individual users, large companies, banks, small startups, doctors, movie makers, and everyone in between. It is more important than ever to be smart about both **security** and **privacy** in the digital age.

---

## Privacy vs Security: What Is the Difference?

These two words are often used together, but they mean different things:

:::tip Key Terms
**Data Privacy** is the appropriate use of your data. When companies and services use information that is provided or entrusted to them, that data should only be used for the agreed purposes.

**Data Security** is the integrity, confidentiality, and availability of your data. It refers to all the practices and processes in place to ensure data is not accessed or used by unauthorised individuals.
:::

In other words: **privacy** is about *who should have access* to your data, while **security** is about *protecting* it so that only the right people can access it.

Think about it this way: a company needs to have good security practices specifically so that it can honour the privacy of the people whose data it holds. Security is the means; privacy is the goal.

## Creating Strong Passwords

Passwords are one of the most important tools for keeping your accounts secure. Research has found that teenagers spend around **9 hours per day** on connected devices, which means many accounts — social media, school portals, email, coding platforms — each needing a secure password.

### What Makes a Password Strong?

Strong passwords are typically:

:::info Features of a Strong Password
- **8–20 characters** in length (the longer, the better)
- A mix of **capital and lowercase letters**
- **Special characters** such as `! @ # $ % ^ & *`
- **Numbers**
- **Completely random** — not full words
- **Different for every account** — so that if one password is compromised, only that one account is affected
:::

### Which Is Stronger?

| Weak Password | Strong Password |
|---|---|
| `password123` | `*pA$s5%w0Rxd86#18` |
| `codehs2018` | `c0#d*3HhSs!()fn` |

The strong passwords are clearly more complex. When creating your own passwords, challenge yourself to avoid familiar words and numbers — the more random, the better.

### Password Tips

- Avoid including personal details such as your birthday, graduation year, or name.
- Do not spell out full words (random characters are harder to crack).
- Do not store passwords in apps like Notes, iMessage, or Evernote — if someone gains access to those apps for even a moment, your passwords can be stolen.
- Writing passwords down or using a personal system to recall them keeps them safe with you.

:::danger Avoid These Password Mistakes
- Using personal information (your name, birthday, or school)
- Reusing the same password across multiple accounts
- Storing passwords in digital notes apps that others could access
:::

## Additional Password Tools

### Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Even a strong password can benefit from an extra layer of protection. **Two-factor authentication** (also called two-step verification) adds a second step to the login process.

:::tip Key Term
**Two-factor authentication (2FA)** is a two-step verification process that adds an extra layer of security to your account beyond just your password.
:::

Examples of 2FA methods include:
- **Texting your phone** with a unique PIN
- **Calling your phone** and reading a unique PIN to you
- **Asking a security question** specific to you — such as the name of the street you grew up on, or the name of your first pet

This means that even if someone discovers your password, they still cannot log in without the second factor.

### Password Managers

A **password manager** is a tool that helps you create, store, and organise complex and diverse passwords.

:::info Password Manager Tips
- Password managers generate strong, random, unique passwords for each of your accounts.
- They store and organise these passwords securely for you.
- You need to remember only **one master password** to access the manager.
- **Keep your master password very safe** — write it down on paper and store it in a secure location like a safe. If you lose it, you will not be able to access your password manager.
:::

## Browsing on Secure Websites

Another important way to stay safe online is to ensure you browse on **secure websites**.

:::tip Key Term
A **secure website** uses the `https://` protocol. The "s" stands for *secure* and means the website uses encryption to protect your data. Websites using just `http://` are less secure.
:::

When visiting websites:
- Always verify that the URL in your browser matches the website you intended to visit — you may have been re-routed to an insecure or fake page.
- Be cautious of websites that produce many pop-ups. One or two may be okay, but use your best judgment.
- If a site seems sketchy or unsafe, it is better to navigate away and browse somewhere else.

---

## Secure Access to Hardware

Security has extended beyond software into your **physical hardware** — your computer or smartphone. Companies and apps often request access to parts of your device including:

- **Webcam** (camera access)
- **Audio and microphone** access
- **Location** access

You might be surprised how many apps and websites have access to your location without you realising it. To check which permissions a website has, you can click the padlock icon next to the `https://` in your browser's address bar and review the site's connection details and settings. It is good practice to do this occasionally, because it is easy to accidentally grant access to your camera or location and then forget about it.

This is another reason why browsing on secure `https://` websites is important — they are more trustworthy when it comes to requesting your hardware permissions.

## Privacy Policies

:::tip Key Term
A **privacy policy** is a legal document that a website must provide, disclosing how it can gather, store, and share your data.
:::

Most people skip reading privacy policies — and that is what companies are counting on. Privacy policies are often intentionally long and dense. However, they are important because by ticking "I agree," you are granting the company permission to use your data in the ways they have outlined.

This may include:
- Selling your information to third parties
- Reading your emails
- Accessing your saved photos
- Targeting advertisements at you based on your behaviour

### Not All Privacy Policies Are Equal

Some privacy policies are designed to use your data to make money or to target you with products and advertisements. Have you ever noticed that a product you searched for on Google later appeared in your Instagram or Facebook feed? That is because your browsing data was tracked and used to serve you targeted advertisements.

:::warning Read Before You Agree
Before agreeing to a privacy policy, it is good practice to at least skim through it. If you cannot understand a company's policy, there are usually online summaries available for major companies. Know what you are agreeing to.
:::

## Avoiding Phishing Scams

:::tip Key Term
**Phishing** is a method used to gather personal information — such as usernames, passwords, credit card numbers, and bank account details — using deceptive emails and websites.
:::

A phishing email may come from an address that looks familiar, but if you look closely there may be a very minor misspelling. The email will then direct you to a fake login page that looks legitimate, where your credentials are stolen as soon as you enter them.

---

## What Can Happen If Your Data Is Stolen?

If a hacker gains access to your accounts or personal data, they could:

- **Impersonate you** on social media
- **Steal money** from your bank account
- **Commit identity theft** — using your information as if they were you
- **Communicate inappropriately** with your colleagues, family members, or friends while pretending to be you
- **Share or sell your private data** to third-party companies

---

## What to Do If You Think You Have Been Hacked

:::warning Act Immediately
If you suspect your account or data has been compromised, do not wait — take action straight away.
:::

1. **Contact a parent or IT professional** at your school immediately.
2. **Contact a representative from the company** (e.g. Gmail, your school portal, Instagram) and explain that the suspicious activity was not you — ask them to freeze your account.
3. **Contact your bank** if you notice any fraudulent charges. Notify them immediately and ask to freeze any compromised debit or credit cards.

Many companies will send you an email if they detect suspicious activity — such as an unfamiliar device or a login from another country. If you receive such a notification and it was not you, act on it immediately.

---

## Security Reminders

:::tip Key Reminders
- Use your best judgment online — whether visiting websites or granting apps access to your hardware.
- Create **secure and unique passwords** for every account.
- **Double-check the URL** of a website before entering any personal information.
- Be especially careful about entering private information on a **public computer** — your details could be saved or seen.
- **Stay up to date** on the best web security practices.
:::

| Data privacy | The appropriate use of your personal data |

| Strong passwords | 8–20 characters; mix of cases, special characters, and numbers; unique per account |
| Two-factor authentication | Adds a second verification step — a PIN sent to your phone or a security question |
| Password managers | Generate and store strong passwords; protect your master password |
| Secure websites | Look for `https://` — the "s" means secure; verify URLs carefully |

| Phishing | Fake emails and websites that trick you into handing over personal information |
