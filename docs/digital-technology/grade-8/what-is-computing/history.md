# Topic 1: History of Computers

## Introduction

What exactly is a computer? Before we trace the history of computing, we need to answer that question. Take a moment to think about it: when you picture a computer, you probably imagine a sleek laptop, a smartphone, or a tablet. But what is it about those devices that actually makes them a computer?

At the core of it, **a computer is a computing machine that can perform calculations according to precise instructions**. Computers take in instructions and then perform mathematical and logical operations. That is really all they are — calculators with a great many added benefits on top. The only thing that separates a computer from a human is speed: a computer can perform very simple mathematical operations millions, even billions, of times per second.

Interestingly, the word "computer" was not originally used to describe a machine at all. In **1613**, the word *computer* was first used to describe a **person** who performed calculations or computations. The first computers were human beings. It is only recently that we have applied the term to the machines that carry out these calculations automatically.

:::tip Key Term
**Computer** — a computing machine that performs calculations according to precise instructions. Computers take in instructions and perform mathematical and logical operations at extremely high speed.
:::

With that definition in mind, let us look back in history and trace where computers came from.

## 1. The Timeline of Computer Development

### 2600 BC — The Abacus

The very first computing machine was the **Chinese abacus**, dating back to around 2600 BC. The abacus was a simple device — a frame with beads on rods — that helped people carry out mathematical operations. It was not automatic; a skilled operator moved the beads to perform calculations. But it was a tool designed to assist with computation, which makes it the earliest computing device we know of.

:::tip Key Term
**Abacus** — one of the oldest computing tools in the world, consisting of beads on rods used to perform arithmetic calculations.
:::

### 1910 — Babbage's Analytical Engine (Partial Construction)

After the abacus, roughly **four thousand years** passed before the next major leap in computing. In **1910**, Henry Babbage (the son of the famous inventor Charles Babbage) constructed part of his father's **Analytical Engine** — a mechanical machine that you hand-cranked. As you turned the crank, it would flip a series of gears and switches and actually perform basic mathematical calculations like addition and subtraction. It was entirely mechanical — no electricity involved.

:::info Did You Know?
The gap between the abacus (2600 BC) and the Analytical Engine (1910 AD) was roughly four thousand years. Yet the next leap — from the Analytical Engine to the first binary digital computer — took only 26 years. The pace of progress accelerated dramatically.
:::

### 1936 — The Z1: The First Binary Digital Computer

In **1936**, German engineer Konrad Zuse built the **Z1** — the first binary digital computer. Unlike the hand-cranked Analytical Engine, the Z1 used **electricity**. More importantly, it used **binary** as its number system — representing all data using only **zeros and ones**. This simplifies things enormously: instead of needing to represent ten different digits (0–9), a machine only needs to represent two states: 0 or 1.

:::tip Key Term
**Binary** — a number system that uses only two digits: 0 and 1. All modern computers use binary because it maps perfectly onto electrical signals — a high voltage represents a 1, and a low voltage represents a 0.
:::

### 1936 — Alan Turing and the Turing Machine Theory

That same year, **Alan Turing** came up with the **Turing Machine** — not a physical machine, but a theoretical concept. Turing imagined a machine that could read and write symbols on a strip of paper, remembering what it had previously seen and writing new symbols on blank sections. By working out what this theoretical machine could and could not do, mathematicians were able to answer fundamental questions about the **limits of computing** — what can be computed by a machine, and what cannot.

This was the foundation of theoretical computer science. Turing did not build a machine; he provided the mathematical framework that proved a universal computing machine was possible.

### 1946 — ENIAC: The First Electronic General-Purpose Computer

In **1946**, the **ENIAC** (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer) was completed. This was the first **electronic general-purpose computer** — meaning it was not pre-programmed to do only one specific task. It could be *reprogrammed* to compute whatever calculation you needed. The military used it to compute trajectories for missiles and to solve other complex problems.

One important historical note: the people who programmed the ENIAC were **all women**. Women were the first computer programmers.

:::info Key Fact
The programmers of the ENIAC were women — they were the first computer programmers in history, operating and programming the machine in the mid-1940s.
:::

### 1950 — UNIVAC: The First Modern Computer

In **1950**, the **UNIVAC** became the first computer that could be called truly "modern" in its design. Instead of requiring an operator to flip physical switches to program it, the UNIVAC had a **keyboard for input** and **disk memory** — it could remember the calculations it had performed. However, even the UNIVAC still occupied an entire room and was only used by businesses and government.

### 1975 — The First Portable Computer

In **1975**, IBM produced the first computer that was not stuck in a room — the first **portable computer**. However, "portable" is relative: it still weighed **55 pounds** (about 25 kg), had a tiny **5-inch display screen**, and had only **64 kilobytes of memory** — less storage than a single song today.

## 2. Moore's Law: Exponential Growth

In just the 50 years since that first portable IBM computer, the advancement in computers has been extraordinary. We now have iPhones, personal computers, tablets, smartwatches, and computers built into almost everything we use. This rapid change is captured by **Moore's Law**.

:::tip Key Term
**Moore's Law** — the observation that the processing speed of a computer (the rate at which it can carry out operations) doubles roughly every two years, as engineers get better and better at making processors. So far, the actual rate of increase has held very close to this prediction.
:::

This is an **exponential** rate of increase. Computers are getting smaller, faster, and cheaper at an accelerating pace. In 50 years, who knows what computers will be able to do.

## 3. Why Were Computers Developed?

The first uses of computers were driven by very practical needs:

- **Counting and trade** — People wanted to keep accurate counts of livestock and sum up money in marketplaces without errors.
- **Astronomy** — Calculating the positions of stars was essential for navigation and for creating accurate calendars.
- **Solving mathematics faster** — As problems became more complex, people needed machines that could perform calculations much more quickly than humans.
- **Military uses** — During wars, computers were used to **decode encrypted messages** and to calculate the correct **trajectory for missiles**.
- **Business uses** — Computers could **automate the production of goods**, keep track of **financial data**, **customer data**, and other large collections of information.

These practical needs drove the development of faster and smaller computers.

## 4. What Do Modern Computers Look Like?

You are probably familiar with modern computers — laptops, desktops, and tablets. But the following are also computers:

- Smartphones
- Smartwatches
- Toys
- Cars
- Kitchen appliances

Computers are found in almost everything in modern life. The only real difference between a general-purpose computer (like a laptop) and a specialised computer (like a coffee maker) is the **programming**.

A **program** is a set of instructions written by a human that is given to a computer to tell it what to do. A laptop is general purpose — you can write programs on it to solve almost any problem. A coffee maker comes pre-packaged with a specific program that tells it exactly what to do when you press a button. Both are computers.

:::tip Key Term
**Program** — a set of instructions written by a human and given to a computer to tell it what to do. Everything a computer does is driven by a program.
:::

## 5. What Are We Using Computers For Today?

The applications of computers in the modern world are almost limitless. Some key examples include:

| Application Area | How Computers Are Used |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| **Transport** | Autopilot systems in planes and cars; self-driving vehicles |
| **Medicine** | Defibrillators, pacemakers, monitoring disease outbreaks |
| **Art and Music** | Creating, visualising, sharing, and performing digital art and music |
| **Agriculture** | Monitoring crop production, predicting future yields |
| **Connection** | Social media, messaging, news — connecting people worldwide |

We are more connected today than we have ever been in human history, and that is entirely thanks to computers.
