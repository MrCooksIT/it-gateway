# Internet Safety

Grade 8 covered the basics of staying safe online. In Grade 9 we examine more sophisticated threats — phishing variants, social engineering, mobile security, and the systems and laws in place to protect you.

## Recap: Core Principles

Personal information you should **never** share online:
- Full name, home address, school name and location
- Phone number and ID/passport number
- Passwords and PINs
- Banking details or card numbers
- Real-time location or daily routine
- Intimate photos or videos

## Advanced Phishing Techniques

Standard phishing uses mass emails hoping someone clicks. Advanced attackers are more targeted.

### Spear Phishing

:::tip Key Term
**Spear phishing** is a targeted phishing attack directed at a specific individual or organisation, using personal information to make the message seem more credible.
:::

An attacker who knows your name, school, and teacher's name can send an email saying: *"Hi [your name], your IT teacher Mr [correct name] has asked you to reset your school account password using this link..."*

This is far more convincing than a generic email.

### Smishing (SMS Phishing)

Phishing via SMS messages. Common examples:
- "Your SARS refund of R2 340 is ready. Click to claim: [link]"
- "Your FNB account has suspicious activity. Verify now: [link]"
- "You've won a Shoprite voucher. Collect here: [link]"

### Vishing (Voice Phishing)

Phishing via phone call. An attacker calls pretending to be a bank, government official, or tech support. They use social engineering to pressure you into revealing account numbers, passwords, or OTPs.

:::danger
**OTP theft**: Banks send One-Time Passwords (OTPs) to verify transactions. A fraudster will initiate a transaction with your stolen card details and then call you pretending to be the bank, asking you to "confirm" the OTP they just sent. **Never share an OTP over the phone** — a real bank will never ask for it.
:::

### Pharming

Redirecting you from a legitimate website to a fake one that looks identical, even if you typed the correct URL. This is done by attacking the DNS system or your device. The fake site captures your login details.

### Signs of Phishing — Advanced Checklist

| Check | What to look for |
|-------|----------------|
| Sender address | Does the domain match the claimed sender? (paypa1.com ≠ paypal.com) |
| URL on hover | Hover over links — does the URL match what you'd expect? |
| HTTPS | Legitimate sites use HTTPS, but note: **phishing sites can also use HTTPS** — it just means encrypted, not safe |
| Urgency | Real organisations rarely demand action within hours |
| Attachments | Unexpected attachments may contain malware |
| Grammar | Even sophisticated attacks sometimes have subtle errors |

## Social Engineering

:::tip Key Term
**Social engineering** is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or revealing confidential information. It exploits human trust, fear, authority, and urgency rather than technical vulnerabilities.
:::

Common social engineering techniques:

| Technique | How it works |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Pretexting** | Creating a false scenario to gain trust ("I'm from your bank's fraud department") |
| **Baiting** | Leaving a USB drive labelled "Salaries 2024" — curiosity leads someone to plug it in and trigger malware |
| **Tailgating** | Physically following an authorised person into a restricted area |
| **Authority** | Impersonating an IT administrator, police officer, or official to get compliance |
| **Reciprocity** | Doing a small favour to create a sense of obligation before making a bigger request |

## Mobile Device Security

Most people now access the internet primarily through smartphones — making mobile security critical.

**Keep your device secure:**
- Set a strong PIN, password, or biometric lock (fingerprint/face ID)
- Enable "Find My Device" / "Find My iPhone" for remote wiping if stolen
- Keep the operating system updated — updates patch vulnerabilities
- Only download apps from official stores (Google Play, Apple App Store)
- Review app permissions — does a flashlight app really need access to your contacts and microphone?
- Enable remote wipe capability in case your phone is stolen

### App Permissions

Every app you install requests permissions. Be suspicious of apps requesting:
- Microphone access when they don't need audio
- Camera access for a calculator
- Contacts access for a game
- Location access when it's irrelevant to the app's purpose

:::warning
**Malicious apps** exist even in official stores, though less commonly. Read reviews, check download numbers, and verify the developer before installing.
:::

## Public Wi-Fi Dangers

Free public Wi-Fi (coffee shops, shopping centres, airports) is convenient but risky.

**Threats on public Wi-Fi:**
- **Eavesdropping (packet sniffing)**: Someone on the same network can intercept unencrypted data
- **Man-in-the-middle attack**: Attacker positions themselves between you and the Wi-Fi router to intercept all traffic
- **Evil twin attack**: Attacker sets up a fake Wi-Fi hotspot with a name similar to the real one (e.g. "Mugg&Bean_Free" vs the real "MuggAndBean_WiFi")

**How to stay safer on public Wi-Fi:**
- Avoid logging into banking, email, or sensitive accounts
- Look for HTTPS (padlock symbol) in your browser
- Use a VPN if available
- Forget the network after use so your device doesn't auto-reconnect
- Use your mobile data for sensitive transactions instead

### VPNs — Virtual Private Networks

:::tip Key Term
A **VPN** (Virtual Private Network) encrypts your internet traffic and routes it through a server in another location, hiding your IP address and making it much harder for anyone on the network to intercept your data.
:::

VPNs are useful for:
- Security on public Wi-Fi
- Privacy from your ISP
- Accessing content that may be geo-restricted

:::warning
Not all VPNs are trustworthy. Free VPNs often collect and sell your browsing data. If you use a VPN, use a reputable paid service.
:::

## Online Grooming

:::tip Key Term
**Online grooming** is when an adult builds a trusting relationship with a child or young person online with the intent to exploit or abuse them.
:::

**How grooming works (warning signs):**
1. A stranger contacts you with flattery and excessive interest
2. They gradually build trust over days or weeks, asking increasingly personal questions
3. They try to isolate you from your friends and family ("They don't understand you like I do")
4. They introduce sexual topics or request photos
5. They may threaten to share images if you try to stop contact

:::danger
If an adult you've met online is asking for photos, wants to meet in person, asks you to keep your conversations secret, or makes you feel uncomfortable in any way — tell a trusted adult immediately. You can also report to SAPS (South African Police Service) or call Childline SA on 116.
:::

## Reporting Unsafe Content in South Africa

| Organisation | Contact | What they handle |
|-------------|---------|-----------------|
| **SAPS Cybercrime Unit** | www.saps.gov.za / nearest police station | Cybercrimes including hacking, harassment, fraud |
| **Childline SA** | 116 (free, 24/7) | Child abuse, grooming, exploitation |
| **FPB (Film & Publication Board)** | www.fpb.org.za | Illegal content including CSAM |
| **South African Banking Risk Information Centre (SABRIC)** | www.sabric.co.za / 011 847 3000 | Banking fraud and phishing |
| **Internet Service Providers' Association (ISPA)** | www.ispa.org.za | Report illegal online content |
