# Copyright & Intellectual Property

When you write code, design a logo, or compose a song, you've created something. Copyright law protects that creation — and using someone else's work without permission has legal consequences. In IT, where content is freely copyable, understanding intellectual property is essential.

> [!NOTE] Grade 10+
> Copyright, licence agreements, and Creative Commons are introduced in Grade 10. Cybercrime legislation extends into Grade 11 and 12.

---

## Intellectual Property (IP)

**Intellectual property** is a creation of the mind — an invention, design, piece of writing, software, music, or artwork — that is owned by its creator and protected by law.

Types of IP protection:

| Type | Protects | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Copyright** | Creative works (music, art, code, writing) | Life of creator + 50–70 years |
| **Patent** | Inventions, technical processes | 20 years |
| **Trademark** | Logos, brand names, symbols | Renewable indefinitely |
| **Trade secret** | Confidential business information | Indefinitely (while secret) |

---

## Copyright

**Copyright** automatically protects an original creative work from the moment it is created — no registration required. The copyright symbol © indicates protected work, but is not mandatory.

### What copyright protects

- Written works: books, articles, code, scripts
- Music and sound recordings
- Films and video
- Artwork, photographs, and graphics
- Software programs
- Websites and online content

### What copyright gives the owner

The copyright owner has the **exclusive right to**:
- Copy and distribute the work
- Modify or create derivative works
- Perform or display the work
- Sell or license the work to others

### What copyright does NOT protect

- Ideas, concepts, and facts (only the expression of them)
- Works in the public domain (copyright expired)
- Works donated to the public domain by the creator

## Licence Agreements

A **software licence agreement** (EULA — End User Licence Agreement) is the legal contract between the software developer and the user, specifying how the software may be used.

**You do not own purchased software — you licence the right to use it.**

| Licence Type | Cost | Modify source? | Distribute? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Proprietary / Commercial** | Paid | No | No — cannot copy | Microsoft Office, Adobe Photoshop |
| **Freeware** | Free | No | Usually yes (as-is) | VLC, 7-Zip (some versions) |
| **Shareware** | Free trial | No | Share trial version | WinZip (trial period) |
| **Open source** | Usually free | Yes | Yes, under conditions | Linux, Firefox, LibreOffice |
| **Public domain** | Free | Yes | Yes, no restrictions | Some old government works |

## Creative Commons (CC)

**Creative Commons** provides a set of standard licences that allow creators to share their work with specific conditions — without others needing to ask permission for each use.

The creator chooses which rights to retain and which to grant:

| Symbol | Term | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **BY** | Attribution | Must credit the original creator |
| **SA** | Share-Alike | Derivative works must use the same licence |
| **NC** | Non-Commercial | Cannot use for commercial purposes |
| **ND** | No Derivatives | Cannot modify or remix the work |

**Common CC licence combinations:**

| Licence | Code | What it allows |
|---|---|---|
| CC BY | Attribution only | Free to use, modify, share — must credit |
| CC BY-SA | Attribution + Share-Alike | Use and modify — but must share under same licence |
| CC BY-NC | Attribution + Non-Commercial | Use for non-commercial purposes only |
| CC BY-NC-ND | Most restrictive CC | Credit only — no commercial use, no modifications |
| CC0 | Public domain dedication | Completely free — no conditions |

## Piracy

**Software piracy** is the illegal copying, distribution, or use of software without authorisation.

**Types of piracy:**
- **End-user piracy**: Installing software on more computers than the licence allows
- **Counterfeiting**: Producing and selling fake copies of commercial software
- **Internet piracy**: Downloading or distributing software via torrent sites, etc.
- **Hard disk loading**: Pre-installing unlicensed software on computers being sold
- **OEM abuse**: Using OEM software outside its intended distribution channel

**Consequences of piracy:**

| For the individual | For the organisation |
|---|---|
| Criminal charges (fines, imprisonment) | Substantial corporate fines |
| Civil lawsuit by software company | Reputational damage |
| Malware risk (pirated software often contains viruses) | Loss of business licence |

---

## Plagiarism

**Plagiarism** is presenting someone else's work — writing, code, ideas — as your own without attribution.

- Copying a classmate's code without credit
- Submitting someone else's essay as your own
- Using online content without referencing the source

> [!NOTE] Plagiarism vs Copyright Infringement
> Plagiarism is an ethical/academic offence (taking credit for others' work). Copyright infringement is a legal offence (using protected work without permission). Both can occur simultaneously — e.g. copying a published article without credit or permission.

---

## Digital Rights Management (DRM)

**DRM** is technology used to control how digital content (music, film, e-books, software) is used, copied, and distributed.

- Prevents unauthorised copying
- Limits number of devices content can be played on
- May require online activation

**Examples:** iTunes purchases (historically), Kindle books, Netflix streaming

**Controversy:** DRM frustrates legitimate users but is often easily defeated by pirates.

## South African Legislation

| Act | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Copyright Act (98 of 1978)** | Protects original creative works |
| **Electronic Communications and Transactions Act (ECT Act, 2002)** | Covers cybercrime, electronic contracts, digital signatures |
| **POPIA (Protection of Personal Information Act, 2013)** | Protects personal data from misuse |

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Copyright** | Automatic legal protection of original creative work |
| **Intellectual property** | Creations of the mind protected by law |
| **EULA** | End User Licence Agreement — terms of software use |
| **Freeware** | Free-to-use software (source code not necessarily open) |
| **Open source** | Software with publicly available, modifiable source code |
| **Creative Commons** | Standardised licences for sharing creative works |
| **Piracy** | Illegal copying or distribution of protected software |
| **Plagiarism** | Presenting others' work as your own |
| **DRM** | Digital Rights Management — technology restricting content use |
| **Public domain** | Works with no copyright restrictions (expired or waived) |

## Exam Focus

> [!IMPORTANT] High-Frequency Questions
>
> 1. **"What is software piracy? Give ONE consequence."** — illegal copying of software; fines, criminal charges, malware risk
>
> 2. **"Explain the difference between freeware and open source software"** — freeware = free to use, closed source; open source = source code available, can modify
>
> 3. **"Describe TWO Creative Commons licence components"** — BY (attribution), SA (share-alike), NC (non-commercial), ND (no derivatives) — always explain what each means
>
> 4. **"Give TWO rights that copyright gives the creator"** — exclusive right to copy, distribute, modify, sell, or licence the work
>
> 5. **"What is an EULA?"** — End User Licence Agreement; legal contract specifying how software may be used; you licence the right to use, not own the software
