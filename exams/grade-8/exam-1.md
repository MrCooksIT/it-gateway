### Section B — Marking Guidelines

**Q11 (3 marks)**
- **RAM (Random Access Memory):** stores currently running applications and the data the computer is actively using; loses all its data when the power is switched off because it requires electricity to maintain its contents. *(1½ marks)*
- **ROM (Read-Only Memory):** stores important startup information such as the BIOS; retains its data permanently, even when the power is off, because it does not need electricity to hold data. Cannot be written to or changed. *(1½ marks)*

**Q12 (3 marks)**
- **Router:** receives data from computers on a network, analyses it, and forwards (routes) it to the correct destination; can also connect to other routers to link different networks together. *(1 mark)*
- **LAN (Local Area Network):** connects computers within a small physical area, such as a single building or school; typically uses a single router. Example: the school computer lab network. *(1 mark)*
- **WAN (Wide Area Network):** connects computers across large distances using multiple routers and long-distance cables; often made up of several LANs connected together. Example: the internet, or a company's network connecting offices in different cities. *(1 mark)*

**Q13 (4 marks)**
- **Hardware** — the physical machine (CPU, discs, mouse, printer); cannot do anything without instructions. *(1 mark)*
- **System software** — programs (including the OS, BIOS, and device drivers) that run on the hardware to manage it and provide a platform for application software; sits between hardware and applications. *(1½ marks)*
- **Application software** — programs the user installs and interacts with directly (games, browsers, word processors); depends on system software to access hardware resources. *(1 mark)*
- Correct description of dependency: application → system software → hardware. *(½ mark)*

**Q14 (3 marks)** — 1 mark each:
- **Persistent:** digital devices allow constant communication, so the bullying can happen at any time, day or night; the victim has no safe space or time to escape it.
- **Permanent:** cyberbullying contributes to the digital footprint of both the victim and the bully; incidents remain as evidence long after the behaviour stops and can follow both parties.
- **Discreet:** teachers and parents cannot see or overhear cyberbullying the way they might witness physical bullying, making it difficult for adults to detect and intervene.

**Q15 (4 marks)** — Award 1 mark per feature + reason (up to 4):
- **Length (8–20 characters):** longer passwords have many more possible combinations, making them harder to guess through brute-force attacks.
- **Mix of uppercase and lowercase letters:** increases the number of possible characters at each position, multiplying the number of combinations an attacker must try.
- **Special characters (! @ # $ % etc.):** adds characters beyond letters and numbers, further increasing the pool of possibilities.
- **Numbers:** similar to above — mixing in digits adds complexity.
- **No full words or personal information:** dictionary attacks try common words and personal details first; random characters cannot be found in a dictionary.
- **Unique per account:** if one password is compromised, only that account is affected; reusing passwords puts all accounts at risk if any single one is breached.

**Q16 (3 marks)**
- **White hat:** hacks with explicit permission from the organisation that owns the system; purpose is to find and report vulnerabilities so they can be fixed (penetration testing). *(1 mark)*
- **Grey hat:** hacks without permission, but typically with no malicious intent; may contact the organisation afterwards and request payment for the information. Still often illegal. *(1 mark)*
- **Black hat:** hacks without permission for personal gain or to cause harm; destroys, steals, or modifies data. *(½ mark)*
- **Key factor:** **permission** — having explicit authorisation to access or test a system is what makes hacking legal. *(½ mark)*

**Q18 (5 marks)**

**(a) 2 marks:**
- Sipho's actions are **not legal** — accessing a system (even without modifying data) without permission is illegal. *(1 mark)*
- Sipho would be classified as a **grey hat hacker** at best — he accessed the system without permission and shared the vulnerability with friends rather than reporting it responsibly. The key principle is that **permission** determines legality, and Sipho had none. His intent being non-malicious is not a legal defence. *(1 mark)*

**(b) 1 mark:**
- Sipho should have reported the vulnerability **immediately to an IT administrator or teacher** at the school, so it could be fixed. He should **not** have accessed any records or shown anyone else. (Accept any answer that correctly identifies responsible disclosure to the appropriate authority.)

**(c) 2 marks:**
- Under laws such as the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (US equivalent), **accessing a computer system without authorisation is itself a crime**, regardless of whether data is stolen or damaged. *(1 mark)*
- The consequences can include felony charges, imprisonment of up to 20 years, and fines of up to $15,000 — in addition to any other charges. "Not stealing" does not exempt someone from these penalties; the unauthorised access itself is the offence. *(1 mark)*
